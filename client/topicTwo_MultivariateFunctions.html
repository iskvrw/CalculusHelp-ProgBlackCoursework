Multivariate Functions

Partial Derivatives

For a function with more than one input, you must choose one of those inputs to differentiate with respect to, meaning that you can only "partially" differentiate for a multivariate function.

For a function \(f(x,y) \), \((x_0,y_0) \) The partial derivative with respect to x at a point is:

\(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} (x_0,y_0) = \lim_{x \to x_0} \frac{f(x,y_0) - f(x_0,y_0)}{x-x_0} \)

Example

Given \(f(x,y)= 2x^2y^3 - xy + 2x - 3y + 5\), the partial derivative of \(f \) wrt to \(x \) is \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 6xy^2 - x - 3 \)

Alternative Notation

You may also see \(f_x \) used when referring to partial derivatives, such as \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \).

Directional Derivatives

Consider a point \(\boldsymbol{x_0} = (x_0, y_0) \) and a vector \(\boldsymbol{v} = (v_x, v_y)^T \). Then, the directional derivative of \(f \) in direction \(\boldsymbol{v} \) is:

\(\nabla_v f = \boldsymbol{v} . (f_x, f_y)^T \)

The symbol \(\nabla f \) is a vector pointing in the direction of greatest rate of increase of \(f \).
