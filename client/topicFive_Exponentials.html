Exp. (e), Logs, and Hyperbolic Functions

Exponential, logarithmic and hyperbolic functions

Eucler's number

Euler's number is often simply referred to as e is equal to the limit \(\lim_{h \to \infty} (1 + \frac{1}{n}) \).The limit forms an increasing sequence, remaining bounded above by 3, and consequently converges to a limit – that is e.

The number e is approximately 2.71828 .

Derivative of ex

\(\frac{d}{dx} (e^x) = e^x \)

Natural Logarhithm

The natural logarithm \(\ln{x} \) or \(\log_e{x} \) is the inverse function of \(e^x\)

Trigonometric Functions

Derivative of the Natural Logarhithm

If \(y = \ln{x} \) ,then \(x=e^x \). Differentiating using the chain rule:

\(\frac{dy}{dx} e^y = 1\)

Rearranging the above gives \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{e^y} = \frac{1}{x} \)

Hyperbolic Functions

Given the unit hyperbola x 2 - y 2 = 1 , the hyperbolic functions are defined by the coordinates of a point at which the area \(\alpha \) has been swept out.

The following formulae can be derived from it:

\(\sinh{\alpha} = \frac{e^{\alpha} - e^{- \alpha}}{2} \)

and

\(\cosh{\alpha} = \frac{e^{\alpha + e^{- \alpha}}}{2} \)
